## Introduction

The primary survey is a quick emergency physical examination that evaluates body systems that are critical to maintain life. It is basically answering the question:

<blockquote style="font-weight:bold">
What is going to kill my patient first?
</blockquote>

The primary survey should be quick (1-2 minutes) and structured (so nothing important gets missed).

There are two described approaches to the primary survey.

A modified approach in humans is (C)ABCDE, where the first C stands for "catastrophic bleeding". If arterial bleeding is present, it should be addressed before everything else.

## Extended Primary Survey, Point-of-Care Diagnostics

Point-of-care diagnostics can provide very important information for the diagnosis, treatment and prognosis of the patient.

While many people consider them part of the primary survey, they do take time and extend the primary survey considerably.

### Emergency Minimum Database

<details>
  <summary><b>PCV/TS & Bloodsmear</b></summary>
  <table>
    <tr>
      <td>Packed cell volume (PCV)</td>
      <td>Rule out anemia, hemoconcentration due to dehydration</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Total solids (TS, aka TP)</td>
      <td>Low TS but normal PCV in a trauma patient is indicative of bleeding.<br>High TS might indicate dehydration.<br>Evaluate oncotic pressure.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Bloodsmear</td>
      <td>Rule out thrombocytopenia with a manual platelet count. Agglutination may be present in IMHA cases.</td>
    </tr>
  </table>
</details>

<details>
  <summary><b>Venous blood gas</b></summary>
  <p>Severe acid-base disturbances is life-threatening and must be corrected.</p>
  <p>Metabolic acidosis is common.</p>
</details>

<details>
  <summary><b>Electrolytes</b></summary>
  <p>Severe eletrolyte derrangements is life-threatening and must be addressed.</p>
  <p>Hypokalemia requiring potassium supplementation is common in severely ill patients.</p>
  <p>Hypernatremia is an indicator of dehydration.</p>
</details>

<details>
  <summary><b>Lactate</b></summary>
  <p>Lactate is an indicator of perfusion. > 2.5 mmol/L is abnormal.</p>
</details>

<details>
  <summary><b>Glucose</b></summary>
  <p>Hypoglycemia is suggestive of sepsis.</p>
  <p>Severe hyperglycemia is suggestive of diabetes / diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).</p>
</details>

<details>
  <summary><b>BUN</b></summary>
  <p>High creatinine/urea — dehydration or AKI/CKD?</p>
</details>

### Others (Situational)

<details>
  <summary><b>Parvoviral testing</b></summary>
  <p>Indicated for all young dogs (< 6 months old) with vomiting and/or diarrhoea (with or without hematochezia), especially if unvaccinated or lives in a region with a high parvoviral case load.</p>
  <p>Usually an ELISA or SNAP test using faecal sample, and takes ~ 10 minutes to run.</p>
  <p>Testing is generally done <i>outside</i> the clinic, so that if it does test positive, the patient is admitted straight into the isolation/infectious ward.</p>
</details>

<details>
  <summary><b>Clotting panel</b></summary>
  <p>Indicated for all bleeding patients, where the cause of the bleeding is unknown.</p>
  <p>Ideally a point-of-care prothrombin time (PT) AND activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT). If only one test is available (perhaps due to cost), the preference is PT due to factor VII being the first to be depleted.</p>
  <p>Activated clotting time (ACT) is similar to aPTT, but not as reliable, and does not correlate well with aPTT.</p>
  <p>Point-of-care tests usually have to be run within a certain time period of blood collection, or from a citrate (blue) tube.</p>
  <p>The peripheral veins are usually recommended, due to the risk of prolonged bleeding. Jugular veins are relatively contraindicated but can be used cautiously — hold off well and for 5 minutes!</p>
</details>

<details>
  <summary><b>Urine drug testing</b></summary>
  <p>Indicated for suspected recreational drug toxicity.</p>
  <p>Not validated for use in non-humans. Beware of palse-positives and false-negatives. Cannot definitely rule in or out the ingestion of ilicit drugs, but a positive result would be highly supportive.</p>
</details>

<details>
  <summary><b>Specific blood tests</b></summary>
  <ul>
    <li>FIV/FeLV</li>
    <li>cPLi / fPLi: SNAP or spec cPL/fPL</li>
    <li>CRP</li>
  </ul>
</details>

## Outcomes

<blockquote align="center" style="font-size:120%;padding:20px">
Is the patient stable or unstable?
</blockquote>

<p>
  If <b>stable</b>,
  <ul>
  Option 1) Continue with a <b>secondary survey</b> (thorough physical examination) and a <b>consultation</b>.
  <br>Option 2) The patient has to <b>wait</b>. Assign a <b>triage priority level</b>.
  </ul>
</p>
<p>
  If <b>unstable</b>,
  <ul>
  What are the <b>life-threatening problems, ranked</b>?
  <br>What <b>stabilisation interventions</b> are immediately required?
  <br>Is there a <b>presumptive diagnosis</b>? (It's okay not to have one.)
  <br>What is the <b>prognosis</b> at this moment?
  <br>How much will treatment likely <b>cost</b> for the next 12-24 hours?
  </ul>
</p>

Admit unstable patients into hospital and start stabilising. Further diagnostics might be required. Reassess often. Triage scoring might be helpful.

Stable patients can wait to be seen.
